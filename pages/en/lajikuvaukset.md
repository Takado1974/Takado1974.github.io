<h2 id="bjj">Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ)</h2>

![Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu](images/lajit_bjj.jpg)

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu and its training can historically be divided into three parts: self-defense, mixed martial arts (MMA), and sport forms. The foundation was self-defense, and the effectiveness of the techniques was verified in vale tudo matches. Later, a sport form was developed that is contested with a gi and without strikes or kicks. Today, many people train exclusively for the sport form, and Submission Wrestling (contested without a gi) has also become a significant part of the discipline.

The typical training uniform is a gi (the BJJ gi resembles a judo uniform), but no-gi training (e.g., t-shirt, shorts, and wrestling shoes) is also an important part of our activities. The forms of training—self-defense, MMA, and sport—are emphasized based on each individual's progress and interests.

Despite its intense history and occasionally tough training methods, Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is a very versatile discipline that can be practiced with various goals and very safely. The objective can be versatile exercise in good company, realistic self-defense skills, or success in a competitive career. The discipline is suitable for juniors and adults, men and women—your own attitude is what matters. Brazilian culture flavors the training sessions, which are typically more relaxed than in many other budo disciplines. However, a certain level of order and discipline is required for safety reasons.

### New Members
New members can start BJJ training on a basic course at the beginning of the season by participating in basic technique sessions. Going through the basic techniques takes about 3 months, after which you can participate in advanced sessions. If a practitioner already has a martial arts background, they may, by agreement with the coach, join advanced sessions and, for example, submission wrestling earlier. The mastery of basic techniques cannot be underestimated, as without them, sparring and learning advanced techniques are not very effective. A gi is not required for basic technique sessions (shorts and a t-shirt are sufficient), but its use is recommended.

### Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu at Takado
Takado in Espoo is one of the first, if not the very first, Finnish clubs to begin serious BJJ training. The foundation of Takado's BJJ teachings was Gracie Jiu-Jitsu, and today it is Alliance BJJ. Our head coach, Alexandre Paiva, is one of the founders of the Brazilian Alliance organization. Alexandre Paiva is also a BJJ World Champion (Mundial 1999) and is particularly known for his technical expertise. He has visited Finland to hold camps and has served as a judge in BJJ competitions we have organized. We consider it important to maintain direct contact with the best Brazilian instructors and fighters.

The leader of Takado's BJJ division is Markku Juntunen, an instructor authorized by Alexandre Paiva and the first person in Finland to be awarded a black belt. Takado has been successfully represented in BJJ and submission wrestling competitions, where we have been the best club in Finland in several National Championships. Our practitioners have also participated in MMA matches.

Belt ranks are one measure of progress, but in BJJ, exceptional patience is required in this regard. The discipline has maintained its realism at Takado, and achieving a belt rank requires actual proof of skill. Belt tests are held regularly. Invitations to belt tests and instructions on the required skills are provided well in advance, and more experienced members help in preparing for the test.

### Recent History
Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is now widely known and recognized as an effective combat and self-defense art among serious martial arts practitioners. BJJ came to the attention of the general public in 1993 through the first Ultimate Fighting Championships (UFC) held in the USA, where almost all techniques were permitted (vale tudo). The Brazilian Royce Gracie won the competitions in dominant style despite being smaller than his opponents. Gracie defeated his opponents quickly, effectively, and without causing unnecessary harm. Also noteworthy was the honorable and gentlemanly behavior of the Gracie family representatives, which was not a given in the contradictory and controversial world of MMA.

Representatives of various martial arts were stunned and incredulous—many still are. The world of martial arts has not been the same since the first UFC events. The situation was made even more amazing by the knowledge that the best representatives of the Gracie family had been undefeated in MMA matches until very recent years—practically for over 70 years.

As the techniques, strategies, and training methods developed by the Gracie family and new generations of Brazilians became more widely known, people everywhere sought to study them. New hybrid disciplines and schools have also emerged, attempting to combine features of various martial arts, especially the ground techniques used in BJJ. It is also interesting how MMA has brought traditional wrestling to a new level of appreciation in real combat situations.

<h2 id="ejj">European Jujutsu</h2>

![European Jujutsu](images/lajit_ejj.jpg)

Takado's European Jujutsu (EJJ) is a constantly evolving, versatile, self-defense-oriented martial art. The range of techniques practiced is very broad, including breakfalls, strikes, kicks, weapon techniques, throws, takedowns, joint locks, chokes, and pins. We aim to practice techniques equally from both standing and ground positions. In European Jujutsu, we do not participate in competitive activities, but sparring exercises are nevertheless an important part of practicing the discipline. Our martial art is also an excellent way to improve and maintain fitness.

Our style is named "European Jujutsu" because our main influences until very recent years have come from jujutsu practiced in the European region rather than Japan. In practice, however, it has been good to note that our foundation is fully sufficient for participating in camps for Japanese jujutsu or other martial arts.

Today, the jujutsu practiced at the club is primarily based on English and German jujutsu, but influences have been taken from judo, karate, BJJ, Chinese boxing, and Escrima. Our club is a member of the Finnish Jujutsu Federation.

### Training Times
We hold a "non-stop" basic course in both autumn and spring. Non-stop means you can join at any point during the season. Non-stop basic technique training sessions are on Wednesdays and Sundays. You can try it out a few times for free.

Sessions for more experienced practitioners are on Mondays and Thursdays. You can attend these sessions if you have a yellow belt or a background in other martial arts. Experienced practitioners can also train during the non-stop sessions, which is a gentle way to return to training after a break.

### Starting Training
A person who already holds a belt rank in jujutsu can continue training at our club with their current rank, regardless of the style they previously practiced. In Takado's belt test system, when performing a test for a new rank, all previous ranks are also performed again.

If a person wishing to join our club does not have a prior jujutsu background, their path to the tatami is through our non-stop (basic technique) sessions. Non-stop sessions are suitable for those starting EJJ training for the first time, those restarting after a break, active practitioners, and those practicing jujutsu for fitness.

Each practitioner participates in training at their own risk. We recommend either checking your current leisure-time insurance regarding jujutsu training or purchasing a license from the Karate Federation. If there has been a break in your active physical exercise, checking your current health status with a doctor may be appropriate. When practiced correctly, jujutsu is a demanding but safe form of exercise.

The content and teaching method of the non-stop sessions are designed so that no prior knowledge of jujutsu or previous training is needed. Techniques are taught from the beginning to those participating for the first time, and we aim to continue performing techniques according to each individual's skill level. The goal is for the sessions to be physically demanding, but the training style is relaxed and calm. Techniques performed according to instructions are safe for everyone.

When starting basic technique training in the non-stop sessions, the practitioner's rank is 6th kyu (white belt). Once the skill level is sufficient relative to participating in other sessions, the practitioner may, with the instructor's permission, join other sessions.

The content of the basic technique sessions largely consists of the requirements for the yellow and orange belt tests. However, to achieve a yellow belt, for example, other things must also be practiced. Belt tests are normally held at the end of the autumn and spring seasons.

### Equipment
In non-stop sessions, sweatpants/shorts and a t-shirt are suitable attire. On the tatami, we train with bare, clean feet. We also ask you to avoid wearing jewelry or necklaces during training and to ensure that fingernails and toenails are not too long.

From the beginning, you can also use a judo, BJJ, jujutsu, karate, or taekwondo uniform. Most of our active practitioners use a judo uniform due to its comfort and durability. When a practitioner also starts participating in the more experienced EJJ sessions, a judo uniform or equivalent should be acquired as soon as possible. It is not mandatory to acquire a belt or uniform if the practitioner continues to participate only in basic technique sessions. The official uniform supplier for the Jujutsu Federation is Petlastech.

Pihatörmä has dressing rooms for both men and women, as well as shower facilities. The facilities are locked, but we recommend that you do not leave anything valuable in the dressing rooms.

![Escrima](images/lajit_escrima.jpg)

### What is Sikado?
Sikado is a blend of several disciplines, strongly based on BIFF-Escrima and Wing Chun. The basic idea of the discipline is to react to situations with the necessary technique, which is not limited to a specific discipline or style.

In Sikado, the starting point is a combat situation, not self-defense, and this is very strongly reflected in the training sessions and methods. The style is aggressive and offensive, not restricted by any rules.

In Sikado, training starts with the practitioner and their attitude, and only then with the technique. The training forms are largely sparring situations that develop tactical awareness and reaction ability, whether the situation is unarmed or armed.

We do, of course, also train the techniques utilized in sparring.

Because we do not focus on techniques alone, we also do not have graduations or belt ranks. In training, everyone does the same thing; we do not differentiate based on skill or ability.

As it is a combat discipline, we practice a wide variety of situations. These can include multiple opponents, various weapons (sticks, knives, swords, axes, or anything suitable as a weapon), or combinations of these.
